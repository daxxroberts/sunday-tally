# Small churches don't need to apologize for their size

Sixty-five people feels small next to a church of six hundred. It's easy for a pastor of a smaller congregation to internalize that number as a scoreboard and quietly feel behind. But raw headcount is the wrong number to compare, and once you look at the right one, the story often flips completely.

I spent 13 years in analytics before building software for churches, and one thing held true across every kind of organization I ever measured: totals favor size. Rates favor health. A smaller group with deeper commitment will out-perform a larger group with shallow commitment every time you measure it the right way.

The church of sixty-five has most of its people serving somewhere. The church of six hundred, for all its size, has a much smaller share doing the same. Look only at the raw numbers and the larger church wins by a mile. Look at commitment per person, and the smaller church is the healthier one.

## Why size quietly becomes the wrong scoreboard

It's an easy trap. A bigger number feels like more ministry happening, more impact, more momentum. But a large room with a small share of people actually engaged isn't more alive than a small room where nearly everyone is. It's just louder. The size of a church says how many people are in the building. It says nothing about how many of them are actually all in.

This isn't a case for staying small on purpose. It's a case for measuring the thing that's actually true about your church, instead of the thing that's easiest to compare on a chart with someone bigger.

## Why this gets missed

Because headcount is the number every church already tracks, and it's the number that gets asked about first, at conferences, in casual conversation, in your own head at 2am. Commitment per person takes one extra step nobody built into the habit: dividing what's happening by how many people are actually there to do it. Skip that step, and size will always look like the whole story, even when it's the smallest part of it.

Seeing your church clearly, at whatever size it is, isn't about defending a number to anyone else. It's about knowing honestly how deeply your people are engaged, so you can steward and grow that, rather than chasing a headcount that was never the real measure of health.

## What to do Monday

Count how many people in your church are actively serving somewhere, in any capacity, and divide it by your total attendance. That's your real number, not the one you'd put next to a bigger church's headcount.

If it's high, that's worth celebrating loudly, more than the raw total ever would be. If it's lower than you'd like, that's a real, useful number to work on, one that has nothing to do with how many more people you'd need to feel like you were winning.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a small church actually less healthy than a large one?
Not necessarily, and often the opposite. Raw attendance size says nothing about how engaged the people already there actually are. A smaller church where most people are serving and committed can be considerably healthier than a larger church with a smaller share of engaged members.

### What's a better number than attendance for comparing church health?
The share of attendees actively serving or engaged in some ongoing way, rather than just showing up. Dividing engagement by total attendance, instead of comparing raw totals, reveals commitment in a way headcount alone never can.

### How can a small church use this to stop feeling behind?
Measure and share the commitment rate, not the raw number. A pastor who can say "most of our people are actively serving" is describing something a much larger church often cannot claim, and that's the real story worth telling, celebrating, and building on.
